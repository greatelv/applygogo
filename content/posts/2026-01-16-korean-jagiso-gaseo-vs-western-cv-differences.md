## Why 92% of Applicants Fail the 2026 AI Screening

By 2026, most mid-to-large Korean companies have integrated advanced AI screening tools. These bots don't just "read" your resume; they scan for structural compliance.

### 1. The "HWP" vs. PDF Deadlock
While the world has moved to PDF, the Korean corporate world still clings to the Hancom (.hwp) format for internal documentation. If an HR portal asks for a specific format and you upload a stylized Canva PDF, the AI might fail to parse your data correctly, leading to an instant "Does Not Meet Requirements" flag.

### 2. The Nuance of "Nunchi" in Text
*Nunchi* (the art of sensing others' thoughts/feelings) is built into the Korean language through honorifics. Using "I" (*Na* vs. *Jeo*) incorrectly or failing to use the proper *Jondaemal* (polite form) in your self-introduction signals a lack of cultural integration. Free translation tools like the basic version of ChatGPT or Google Translate often fail this, producing "robotic" Korean that feels cold or unintentionally rude to a native recruiter.

## Workflow: Converting Your Profile for the Korean Market

If you are serious about securing an E-7 visa or a professional role in Seoul, you must stop translating and start **calibrating**.

### Step 1: Audit Your Action Verbs
In the West, we use "Led," "Managed," and "Transformed." In Korea, these can sound overly individualistic.
*   **Wrong:** "Led a team of 10 to increase sales by 20%."
*   **Natural:** "Contributed to a 20% sales increase by fostering team synergy and supporting departmental goals."

### Step 2: The "Professional" Photo
In many Western countries, photos on resumes are a legal liability. In Korea, they are a standard expectation. This isn't about "looks"; it's about presenting a professional image. A selfie or a casual headshot is a disqualifier. You need a formal studio shot with a neutral background.

### Step 3: Handle the Honorifics
If your self-introduction is written in *Banmal* (casual Korean) or a mix of polite/casual, it will be discarded. You must use the "Formal-Polite" style (*-hasipsio-che*) for official documents.
