## 1. The 'Iryeokseo' vs. 'Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo' Gap

In the West, a resume is a marketing brochure. It’s "I did X to achieve Y." You keep it short, punchy, and results-oriented.

In Korea, the recruitment process is built on two distinct documents: the **Iryeokseo (이력서)**, which is a factual summary of your education and dates of employment, and the **Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo (경력기술서)**, or Detailed Career Statement. 

If you only submit a Western-style resume, Korean HR views it as a mere *Iryeokseo*. To them, you’ve provided the "what" and the "when," but you’ve completely omitted the "how" and the "where I fit." Without a *Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo*, you are effectively presenting yourself as a blank slate—leading them to default your seniority to entry-level or junior status because they cannot verify the depth of your organizational influence.

## 2. 'I' vs. 'The System': The Narrative Mismatch

Western resumes are individualistic. They focus on personal agency: *"I increased sales by 20%."* 

In the Korean corporate mindset, especially within the *Chaebol* (conglomerate) ecosystem, "I" is a dangerous word. Korean HR looks for **organizational harmony (Inhwa)** and **process loyalty**. They want to see how you moved within the hierarchy to achieve results. 

A "senior" professional in Korea is someone who can navigate complex internal approvals, manage stakeholders across departments, and mentor juniors within the specific cultural framework of the company. If your resume only talks about your individual numbers, the recruiter sees a "maverick" or a "freelancer"—someone who might be talented but is perceived as too "high-risk" for a senior leadership role. They offer you a junior role because they think you need to be "re-educated" on how to work in a team.

## 3. The 'Sincerity' (Seongsil) Factor and Data Recalibration

When preparing for the Fall 2026 season, you must use the right keywords. In Korea, being "passionate" is common; being **'Seongsil' (성실 - Sincere/Diligent)** is the gold standard. 

But you cannot just say you are sincere. You must prove it through the narrative structure of your career statement. This involves:
- **Chronological Consistency**: Korean resumes often require your education history from high school onwards. Skipping this or listing it in "reverse chronological order" only can confuse traditional HR systems.
- **Project Breakdown**: Instead of one bullet point about a project, a *Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo* requires a table-like breakdown: Project Name, Duration, Your Role, Specific Tools Used, and the Organizational Impact.
- **Honorifics and Formal Language**: Using Google Translate to turn your resume into Korean is a one-way ticket to the rejection pile. A single error in *Jondaemal* (honorifics) suggests a lack of cultural respect, a mistake no "Senior" candidate would ever make.

## 4. Why Doing It Alone is a High-Stakes Gamble

The "DIY" approach to the Korean job market is exhausting and often futile. 
- **The HWP Trap**: Many Korean firms still use Hancom Office (.hwp). If your formatting breaks or you submit a PDF that their legacy ATS (Applicant Tracking System) can't read, you're out.
- **The "Native Smell"**: Korean recruiters can identify a non-native or poorly localized resume in seconds. It’s not just the language; it’s the *spacing*, the *font (Gulim or Nanum)*, and the *way your photo is presented*.

If you are a 10+ year veteran, your time is worth more than struggling with margins in a language you haven't mastered for professional documentation.
