While those factors exist, the most common culprit is something much more structural: **The Gyeongnyeok-Kisulseo (경력기술서) Gap.**

In the eyes of a Korean HR manager in 2026, your sleek, one-page Western CV doesn't prove you have experience—it proves you have a "summary" of experience. To them, it looks shallow, unverified, and ultimately, like "Zero Experience."

## 1. The "What" vs. "How" Dilemma: Why Bullets Aren't Enough

The standard Western resume is built on the "Achievement-First" principle. We use punchy bullet points: *"Increased sales by 20% through a new marketing strategy."* 

In Korea, this is considered insufficient. A Korean recruiter isn't just looking at the result; they are looking for the **technical process and your specific location within the organizational hierarchy.** 

The *Gyeongnyeok-Kisulseo* (Detailed Career Statement) is a separate document or a massive section in the application where you must deconstruct your career into a technical manual. If you simply say you "managed a team," the Korean manager wants to know: 
- What was the exact reporting line? 
- What specific software or methodology (Agile, Waterfall, Six Sigma) was used at every stage? 
- What were the specific constraints of the project?

When you submit a 1-page resume that omits these "How" details, Korean HR assumes you were merely a "participant" rather than an "owner." To bridge this gap, you must transition from a "Result-Oriented" narrative to a "Process-Validating" narrative.

## 2. The Hierarchy Factor: Defining Your Seniority

Korean corporate culture is deeply rooted in rank (Sawon, Daeri, Gwachang, Chajang, Bujang). When an HR manager looks at a candidate with 3 years of experience, they are trying to determine if you function at the level of a *Daeri* (Assistant Manager). 

A Western resume often obscures this by using creative titles like "Growth Rockstar" or "Customer Success Evangelist." These mean nothing to a Korean recruiter. If your resume doesn't clearly map your responsibilities to the expected technical competencies of a Korean *Daeri*, they will default to the lowest risk option: treating you like a *Shinip* (Newcomer).

To fix this, your career statement needs to use the language of **'Seongsil' (Sincerity/Diligence)** and **'Gwonhan' (Authority/Scope)**. You must demonstrate that you handled the administrative, technical, and collaborative burdens associated with mid-level roles in Korea. 

## 3. The 2026 Standard: Why "Google Translate" is a Career Killer

Many applicants think, "I'll just translate my English resume into Korean using AI, and it will be fine." 

**It won't.** 

Korean recruitment in 2026 has become hyper-specialized. Recruiters can spot a translated resume in three seconds. The errors aren't just grammatical; they are cultural.
- **Honorifics (Jondaemal):** Using the wrong level of politeness in your *Jagisogaeseo* (Self-Introduction) signals a lack of cultural integration.
- **Formatting:** Korean resumes often require specific orders (Education from high school onwards, family background in some cases, specific photo dimensions).
- **Keywords:** Westerners love the word "Passionate." In Korea, "Responsible" (Chaegimgam) and "Collaborative" (Hyeop-eop) carry much more weight when backed by data.
