## 1. The Myth of the "One-Page Resume" in Korea

In New York, London, or Berlin, a one-page resume is a sign of efficiency. In Seoul, it’s often perceived as a lack of transparency or effort. While the *Yiryeokseo* (the basic resume) provides a snapshot of your timeline, the **Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo** is where the hiring decision is actually made.

Korean HR managers at firms like Samsung or Hyundai are not just looking for *what* you did; they are obsessed with *how* you did it and the technical environment in which you operated. A Western bullet point like "Increased sales by 20%" is a conversation starter in the US. In Korea, it’s a question mark. Without the "Experience Statement" detailing the specific tech stack, the organizational hierarchy of the project, and the "Process-Oriented" breakdown of your contribution, your application is filtered out by AI-ATS systems before a human even sees it.

## 2. Why Your English Bullet Points Are Failing You

The "Action-Verb" strategy taught in Western career centers often fails to translate into the Korean corporate mindset. Korean recruitment culture values **'Seongsil' (Sincerity/Diligence)** and **'Gieon' (Contribution to the whole)**. 

When you write "Spearheaded a new marketing strategy," a Korean manager reads "Individualist who might not fit into a collaborative hierarchy." To succeed, your narrative must be re-engineered. You need to transition from a "Hero Narrative" to a "Architectural Narrative."

The Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo requires:
- **Project Overviews**: The duration, scale, and budget of your work.
- **Role Specificity**: Not just your title, but your specific coordinates within the team.
- **Technical Depth**: A granular list of tools, methodologies, and 'Troubleshooting' instances that prove you can handle the rigors of a Korean workplace.

## 3. The "Honorific Trap" and the AI-ATS Filter

Even if you realize you need a Korean-style document, using Google Translate or Papago is a death sentence for your application. Korean is a language of hierarchy. Using the wrong level of *Jondaemal* (honorifics) or failing to use professional industry terminology (*Yong-eo*) makes you look amateurish.

Furthermore, Korean companies are increasingly utilizing AI-ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems) specifically tuned for the Korean language. These systems look for "Native Professionalism." If your Experience Statement lacks the specific linguistic markers of a seasoned Korean professional, you are ghosted instantly.
