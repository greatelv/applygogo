## 1. The Death of the One-Page Resume: Understanding 'Jeong-seong'

In New York or London, brevity is the soul of wit. In Seoul, detail is the soul of **Jeong-seong (정성)**—a term that translates to "sincerity" or "devotion."

When a Korean HR manager opens your one-page English CV, they don't see an efficient professional. They see someone who couldn't be bothered to explain their "Growth Process" or their "Motive for Application" in the depth required by the Korean *Jagisogaeseo* (Personal Statement). To them, a short resume implies you are "mass-applying" without a specific interest in their company.

To bridge this gap, you must expand. A successful Korean application typically consists of two distinct parts:
1.  **Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo (Experience Statement):** A multi-page, granular breakdown of every project, tool, and outcome.
2.  **Jagisogaeseo (Personal Statement):** A narrative-driven document that answers culturally specific questions about your upbringing, your philosophy on teamwork, and your long-term loyalty to the firm.

## 2. The Keyword Trap: Why "Passionate" is a Weak Word

Many international applicants fill their resumes with buzzwords like "Passionate," "Self-starter," or "Out-of-the-box thinker." In the Korean context, these are often dismissed as "empty noise."

Korean recruiters look for **Seongsil (성실)**—diligent sincerity. Instead of saying you are passionate, you must demonstrate your *Seongsil* through data and chronological consistency. For example, rather than saying "Passionate about Korean culture," you must prove it by showing "4 years of consistent Topik level 5 attainment while working a full-time job."

Furthermore, the structure of your education and experience must follow the Korean standard: **Reverse-chronological is expected, but the level of detail regarding your high school, university location, and even your family registry (in some traditional firms) remains a hidden cultural nuance that confuses outsiders.**

## 3. The "Google Translate" Kiss of Death

If you think you can simply run your English resume through an AI translator and call it a day, you are handing the recruiter a reason to reject you. 

Korean is a language of hierarchy. The "Honorifics" (Jondaemal) used in a professional resume are vastly different from the language used in a casual setting or generated by generic AI models. A single error in verb endings can make you sound arrogant or uneducated to a veteran HR manager at a company like SK or LG.
