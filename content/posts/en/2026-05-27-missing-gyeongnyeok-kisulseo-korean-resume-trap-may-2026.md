## 1. The Great Misconception: "The Resume is Enough"

In the US or Europe, a one-page resume is the gold standard. It’s a highlight reel designed for a 6-second scan. Many foreign candidates believe that by translating this one-page resume into Korean and attaching a 'Jagisogaeseo' (Self-Introduction), they have covered all bases.

In the Korean corporate ecosystem of 2026, this is a fatal mistake. 

While the *Jagisogaeseo* tells the story of **who you are** (your values, your "growth process," and your fit for the company culture), the *Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo* is a technical deep-dive into **what you have done**. It is not a summary; it is a meticulous, project-by-project breakdown of your professional history. 

Without it, a Korean HR manager at a company like Coupang or Hyundai sees your profile as **"Shallow" or "Unverified."** They don't just want to know you "increased sales by 20%"; they want to know the specific tech stack, the team size, your specific role in the hierarchy, and the exact methodology used over a specific timeline.

## 2. Anatomy of a Winning 'Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo'

In 2026, the demand for global talent in Korea has shifted. Companies no longer hire "global experts" just for their English skills; they hire them to solve specific technical problems. Your *Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo* must reflect this.

A standard Korean Career Description includes:
1.  **Project Period**: Exact dates (YYYY.MM - YYYY.MM).
2.  **Organization/Client**: Who was the work for?
3.  **Project Name & Goal**: What was the primary objective?
4.  **Key Role & Tech Stack**: Were you the Lead Developer? The PM? Did you use Python, AWS, or React?
5.  **Specific Outcomes (Quantified)**: KPIs, revenue growth, or efficiency gains.

For a Korean recruiter, this document acts as a "Proof of Capability." In a culture that values *Seongsil* (sincerity/diligence) and *Jeonmunseong* (professionalism), a vague Western resume looks like you are hiding a lack of depth. If you don't provide this document, they won't ask for it—they will simply move on to the next candidate who has provided a 3-page detailed breakdown.

## 3. The 2026 "Cultural Smell Test"

Korean HR managers can "smell" a non-native resume from a mile away. It isn't just about the grammar; it's about the **formatting and honorifics**. 

Using Google Translate or even generic AI to draft your documents often results in "awkwardly polite" or "unintentionally rude" phrasing. For example, failing to use the correct business terminology for "resigned" or "promoted" can make you look like an outsider who hasn't bothered to learn the local business etiquette.

Furthermore, Korean resumes often require information that Westerners find intrusive or irrelevant—such as the specific order of your education history (often from high school onwards) or specific certifications that must be listed in a very particular HWP or PDF layout.
