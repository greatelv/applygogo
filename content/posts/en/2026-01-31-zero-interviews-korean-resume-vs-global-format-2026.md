## 1. The 'Iryeokseo' vs. The 'Resume': It’s Not Just a Translation

Most foreigners believe that translating their English resume into Korean is enough. It isn't. In the West, a resume is a marketing brochure—a highlight reel of your best moments. In Korea, the standard **'Iryeokseo' (이력서)** is a structured ledger of your life.

Even in 2026, many Korean conglomerates and mid-sized firms expect a table-based format. They want to see your education history in reverse chronological order, often starting from high school. They look for specific certifications, military service status (even for foreigners, to understand your visa/legal standing), and sometimes even a professional headshot—a practice that is fading but still lingers in the subconscious of traditional hiring managers.

When you submit a 1-page "modern" resume, a Korean recruiter sees a lack of detail. They see someone who doesn't respect the local standard. They see a "flight risk" who hasn't bothered to learn how Korea does business.

## 2. The 'Jagisogaeseo': The Narrative That Wins the Interview

This is the biggest hurdle for 99% of foreign applicants. In the West, we write Cover Letters. In Korea, we write **'Jagisogaeseo' (자기소개서)**, or 'Jaso-seo' for short.

A Cover Letter is about why you want the job. A Jagisogaeseo is a four-pillar psychological profile that usually includes:
1.  **Growth Process (성장과정):** How your upbringing shaped your values.
2.  **Personality Pros & Cons (성격의 장단점):** A humble but strategic look at your character.
3.  **Motivation for Application (지원동기):** Why *this* specific company, not just any company.
4.  **Aspiration after Joining (입사 후 포부):** Your concrete 5-year plan within the firm.

Foreigners often fail here because they focus too much on "I did X." Korean recruiters are looking for **'Seongsil' (성실)**—a combination of sincerity, diligence, and loyalty. If your narrative doesn't demonstrate how you overcame hardship or how your personal values align with the company's "founding spirit," your technical skills won't matter.

## 3. The Honorifics Trap: Why Google Translate is Your Enemy

In 2026, AI translation has improved, but it still fails the "Vibe Check." Korean is a hierarchical language. A resume written in the wrong level of honorifics (Jondaemal vs. Banmal) is an instant disqualification. 

I recently spoke with an HR manager at a major Korean gaming company. He told me, *"I can tell within three seconds if a resume was written by a human who understands Korea or a machine. Machine-translated resumes feel 'dry' and often use awkward formal endings that make the candidate sound like a robot or, worse, arrogant."*

If you are applying for a role where you will work with Korean teams, your resume is your first test of cultural fluency. If you fail the document stage, they will never trust you to handle a client meeting in Seoul.
