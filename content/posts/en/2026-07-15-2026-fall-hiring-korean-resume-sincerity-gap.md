## 1. The 'Jeongseong' Gap: Why Brevity is Your Enemy

In New York, London, or Berlin, a two-page resume is often considered "too long." In Seoul, a one-page resume is considered "empty." 

Korean recruiters operate under a different cultural mandate. They aren't just looking for your technical skills; they are looking for your fit within the corporate hierarchy and your level of dedication to the specific company. This is where the concept of *Jeongseong* comes in. When a Korean HR manager opens a 1-page PDF, they don't see "concise and professional." They see a candidate who likely "copy-pasted" a generic resume to a hundred different companies.

To pass the screening (Seoryu-jeonhyeong), you must provide two critical documents that go far beyond a standard CV:
1.  **Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo (경력기술서):** A deep-dive Career Description that breaks down every project, your specific role, the tools used, and the quantitative results.
2.  **Jagisogaeseo (자기소개서):** The "Self-Introduction Letter," which is a multi-part essay that covers your growth process, personality, and specific motivation for joining.

## 2. The 4 Pillars of the 'Jagisogaeseo'

If you want to survive the 2026 Fall hiring season, you need to master the narrative structure of the Korean Self-Introduction. Most global candidates fail because they treat these questions as "optional fluff." They are not. They are the most scrutinized part of your application.

### A. The Growth Process (성장과정)
Westerners often find this invasive. Why does a tech company care about your childhood? They don't care about your hobbies; they care about the **values** you formed. Did you overcome a significant hardship? Did you learn the value of 'Seongsil' (sincerity/diligence) through a specific life event? In Korea, your past is a predictor of your loyalty.

### B. Strengths and Weaknesses (성격의 장단점)
Avoid the "I'm a perfectionist" cliché. Korean HR looks for how your strengths align with "In-jae-sang" (the company’s ideal talent image) and, more importantly, how you proactively manage your weaknesses to ensure they don't affect team harmony.

### C. Application Motive (지원동기)
This must be hyper-specific. Why this company? Why now? If you don't mention the company's 2026 quarterly goals or their recent expansion into specific markets, you haven't done your homework.

### D. Post-Hiring Goals (입사 후 포부)
Don't just say "I want to help the company grow." Give a 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year roadmap. Show them that you have a plan to integrate and contribute to the Korean organizational culture.

## 3. The Technical Trap: Honorifics and Formatting

Even if your content is great, "Western-style" formatting can kill your chances. 
*   **The HWP vs. PDF Battle:** While many tech startups accept PDFs, conservative conglomerates still live and breathe Hancom Office (.hwp). Submitting a broken Word doc is an instant rejection.
*   **The Linguistic Nuance:** Translating "I managed a team" into Korean isn't just about the words. It's about using the correct **Honorifics (Jondaemal)**. If your resume uses an informal or slightly "off" tone, you appear arrogant or uneducated in the eyes of a traditional Korean manager.
*   **Photo and Personal Info:** While "Blind Recruitment" is growing, many companies still expect a professional headshot and specific personal details that Western resumes omit for privacy. Knowing when to include these is key to "looking the part."
