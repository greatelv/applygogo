## 1. The "Context Gap": Why Bullets Aren't Enough

In the West, we are taught that "less is more." We use punchy action verbs: *“Increased revenue by 20%.” “Managed a team of 10.”* We assume the reader understands the Herculean effort behind those numbers.

In Korea, the **Gyeongnyeok Kisulseo (Detailed Career Statement)** is the gold standard. A Korean recruiter doesn't just want the result; they want the **process, the reporting line, and the administrative scope.** 

When a Korean HR manager sees a 1-page English resume, they don't see a "high-performer." They see someone who hasn't provided enough evidence of organizational "seniority weight." Without a detailed breakdown of your role within the corporate hierarchy—using specific terminology like *Chong-hwal* (General Management) or *Ki-haek* (Strategic Planning)—your 10 years of experience are literally lost in translation. They default to a junior ranking because they cannot "verify" the seniority of your previous roles.

## 2. The Language of Authority: Beyond "Passionate"

One of the biggest mistakes global talents make is using "soft" descriptors. Foreigners often describe themselves as "passionate," "creative," or "hard-working." In the Korean corporate mindset, these are traits of an entry-level *Sinib* (Newcomer).

To be viewed as a senior, your resume must speak the language of **Seongsil (Sincerity/Consistency)** and **Chaeg-im (Responsibility).** But it goes deeper than that. You need to map your career using the "Senior-level" Korean vocabulary that denotes authority:

*   **Pum-ui (Internal Approval):** Did you have the authority to draft and finalize internal proposals?
*   **Seong-gwa Gwan-ri (Performance Management):** How did you evaluate your subordinates?
*   **Sijang-jeomyu-yul (Market Share):** How did your specific strategic decisions impact the company's macro-position?

If you aren't using these terms, your profile lacks the "gravitas" required for a senior position. At **ApplyGoGo**, we see thousands of resumes where "Led a marketing campaign" is translated into a simple Korean verb. In reality, it should be re-engineered into: *"Spearheaded the integrated marketing strategy and secured executive-level approval for a 500M KRW budget (Pum-ui), resulting in a measurable increase in market share."*

## 3. The June "Susi Chaeyong" Rush: Why Formatting is Your Enemy

June is a critical month. As we hit the mid-year mark, Korean firms move away from mass hiring and focus on **Susi Chaeyong (Rolling Recruitment)** to fill specific senior gaps. These roles are filled fast. 

If your resume is in a standard PDF format that doesn't follow the Korean visual structure (Chronological education starting from high school, specific family registry formatting where applicable, and the 4-pillar Jagisogaeseo), the recruiter will move on in exactly 6 seconds. 

They aren't being mean; they are busy. They need to see your "Professional Fit" immediately. If they have to do the mental gymnastics of converting your "Manager" title in New York to the equivalent "Cha-jang" or "Bu-jang" level in Seoul, they will simply choose the candidate who already did the work for them.
